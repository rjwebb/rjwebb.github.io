Noigandres Group and Brazilian Concrete Poetry

The theoretical foundations of concrete poetry, as articulated by one of its Brazilian practitioners, were laid out in Décio Pignatari's article "Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline," originally published in ad–arquitetura e decoração, São Paulo, ano IV, n. 2, March–April 1957.[1]

Theoretical Precursors

Mallarmé

Within this framework, Stéphane Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dès (1897) is identified as his last, impressive work.[2] It is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.[3] The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of "prismatic subdivisions of the Idea" cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry.[4] Realizing those problems required using page space as an element of structure — deploying words in different typefaces, cases, and reading indications — so that the page becomes a verbivocovisual score.[5] In the context of concrete poetry's intellectual genealogy, Un Coup de Dès occupies a role analogous to that of Anton Webern for electronic music,[6] to that of Maliévitch or Mondrian for concrete painting,[7] and to that of Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier for modern architecture.[8] (See also Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry.)

Ezra Pound and the Ideogram

Ezra Pound gave an elementary but clear idea of the ideogram based on Ernest Fenollosa's studies of Chinese writing.[9] The Chinese define the color red with abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo;[10] according to Pound, the Chinese ideogram for red is based on things everyone knows.[11] Concrete poetry, however, goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Pound,[12] introducing space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure[13] and thereby creating a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality.[14] In doing so, it destroys traditional linear rhythm.[15]

James Joyce

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible.[16] Joyce achieves a small verbivocovisual ideogram in each of his metaphorwords — for example, "silvamoonlake" — which plays on the Latin silva (forest) and the orthographic-phonetic representation of "silver-silva."[17] This example illustrates the "panaroma of all flores of speech" that Joyce began in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake.[18] However, Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition.[19]

Apollinaire and Visually Oriented Poetry

Visually oriented poetry can be found across a range of antecedents, including Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists.[20] The first attempt to systematize and theorize on the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire with his calligrams.[21] Those calligrams, however, demonstrate in their failure the insufficiency of his procedure.[22] Apollinaire also used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.[23]

Fontana and Epigenetic Poetry

Related developments in experimental poetics involved the Italian poet Fontana, whose work is distinguished by the confluences of several artistic disciplines[24] and by the intersections of languages.[25] Contamination always permeates Fontana's poetic work,[26] and the work aims at poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of its elements.[27] In Fontana's poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles.[28]

Fontana assigns a fundamental structural role to the voice,[29] which mediates and catalyzes,[30] and which forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes.[31] Fontana's poetic project has roots in experiments of sound poetry that began in the late 1960s[32] and is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing.[33] In this project, vocalism and writing interact with each other, creating continuously transforming areas of intervention,[34] and electronics occupies all but negligible spaces in these areas.[35]

Fontana's writing produces pre-texts rather than texts.[36] Pre-texts are places to transfigure acoustically and visually,[37] territories of action to re-frame beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms,[38] and they generate writings in motion and poetic performances[39] that are capable of re-generating further writings.[40] Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has been sustaining new functions in writing that lead to the conception of text as integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages.[41]

In the work Poema Larsen (1983), the use of the microphone aims at producing and articulating sound, not merely amplifying it,[42] and the sound is the fruit of a relation between voice, gesture, and geometric space.[43] The text in Poema Larsen is limited to the word "microphone," fragmented in its phonematic components, and acts as a percussor of masses of air to put in vibration the acoustic space.[44] The only word used is the pretext to trigger the Larsen effect, which is modulated by the action,[45] and each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds.[46]

The poet in this paradigm transforms into a poly-artist who takes possession of electronic, videographic, cinema, photography, sonorous universe, theatrical dimension, and rhythmic universe,[47] acting poetically using all techniques, supports, and spaces, without renouncing body, gesture, and voice, which constitute the basis of a new poetic attitude.[48] The poly-artist is the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry,[49] and the poetic body undergoes subsequent processes of reorganization and progressive plastic modeling — a condition that may be described as Epigenetic Poetry.[50]
