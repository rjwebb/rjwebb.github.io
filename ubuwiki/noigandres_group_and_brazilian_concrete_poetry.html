Noigandres Group and Brazilian Concrete Poetry

Noigandres Group and Brazilian Concrete Poetry

Noigandres Group and Brazilian Concrete Poetry is a subject concerning concrete poetry and its use of space as a substantive element in poetic structure, producing a new rhythmic and spatial-temporal reality.[^c_07485b_0006][^c_07485b_0007]

Structural and spatial principles

The article “Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline” was originally published in ad–arquitetura e decoração, São Paulo, year IV, no. 2, in March–April 1957.[^c_07485b_0001] Its account presents concrete poetry as extending the ideogrammic process beyond its application by Ezra Pound, introducing space into the ideogram as an element of poetic structure.[^c_07485b_0005][^c_07485b_0006] This spatial organization creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality and destroys traditional linear rhythm.[^c_07485b_0007][^c_07485b_0008]

Mallarmé

Stéphane Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés (1897) is described as his last major work and as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.[^c_07485b_0014][^c_07485b_0015] The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” are presented as covering the principal areas of concern to concrete poetry.[^c_07485b_0016]

Their realization required the use of page space as an element of structure, including words in different typefaces and cases and the use of reading indications.[^c_07485b_0017] The page thereby becomes a verbivocovisual score.[^c_07485b_0017] Un Coup de Dés is compared with Anton Webern in electronic music, with Kazimir Malevich and Piet Mondrian in concrete painting, and with Walter Gropius, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, and Le Corbusier in modern architecture.[^c_07485b_0018][^c_07485b_0019][^c_07485b_0020]

Pound and the ideogram

Pound’s account of the ideogram is described as an elementary but clear idea based on Ernest Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing.[^c_07485b_0002] One example states that Chinese signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo are used to define the color red.[^c_07485b_0003] According to Pound, the Chinese ideogram for red is therefore based on things familiar to everyone.[^c_07485b_0004]

Concrete poetry extends this process by making space a substantive element of poetic structure.[^c_07485b_0005][^c_07485b_0006] Its spatial organization produces a new rhythmic and spatial-temporal reality rather than preserving traditional linear rhythm.[^c_07485b_0007][^c_07485b_0008]

Joyce

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible.[^c_07485b_0009] Joyce produces a small verbivocovisual ideogram in each of his metaphor-words, including “silvamoonlake.”[^c_07485b_0011] The word combines the Latin silva, meaning forest, with the orthographic-phonetic representation of “silver-silva.”[^c_07485b_0012]

“Silvamoonlake” exemplifies the “panaroma of all flores of speech” begun by Joyce in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake.[^c_07485b_0013] Unlike the spatial procedure described for concrete poetry, Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition.[^c_07485b_0010]

Visually oriented poetry

Visually oriented poetry occurs in works associated with Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists.[^c_07485b_0021] Guillaume Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem through his calligrams.[^c_07485b_0022] The calligrams are also described as demonstrating, through their failure, the insufficiency of that procedure.[^c_07485b_0023] Apollinaire used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.[^c_07485b_0024]
