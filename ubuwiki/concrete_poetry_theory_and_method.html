Concrete Poetry: Theory and Method

Concrete poetry developed a distinctive theoretical framework centred on the restructuring of the page, the word, and the relationship between visual, sonic, and verbal dimensions of language.

The Ideogrammic Method and Its Extension

Ezra Pound gave an elementary but clear idea of the ideogram based on Fenollosa's studies of Chinese writing. [1] The Chinese define the color red with abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo, [2] and according to Pound, the Chinese ideogram for red is based on things everyone knows. [3] Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Pound: [4] it introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure, [5] creating a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality [6] and destroying traditional linear rhythm. [7]

Mallarmé and the Space-Time Foundation

Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dès (1897) is his last, impressive work. [8] It is the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [9] The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of "prismatic subdivisions of the Idea" cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. [10] The realization of those problems required using page space as an element of structure — with words in different typefaces, cases, and reading indications — making the page a verbivocovisual score. [11] Un Coup de Dès represents for concrete poetry what Anton Webern represents for electronic music, [12] what Maliévitch or Mondrian represents for concrete painting, [13] and what Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier represents for modern architecture. [14] (See also [e_mallarme_and_concrete_poetry].)

The Verbivocovisual Ideal and Joyce

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. [15] Joyce achieves a small verbivocovisual ideogram in each of his metaphorwords — for example, silvamoonlake, [16] which plays on the Latin silva (forest) and the orthographic-phonetic representation of silver-silva. [17] This example illustrates the "panaroma of all flores of speech" which Joyce began in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake. [18] However, Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition. [19]

Precursors in Visual Poetry

Visually oriented poetry can be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the Futurists. [20] The first attempt to systematize and theorize on the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire with his calligrams. [21] Apollinaire's calligrams, however, demonstrate in their failure the insufficiency of his procedure. [22] Apollinaire used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin. [23]

Fontana's Epigenetic and Hyper-Poetic Extensions

One important theoretical extension of concrete poetry's core concerns appears in the work of Fontana, whose poetic project is distinguished by the confluences of several artistic disciplines [1] and by the intersections of languages. [24] Contamination always permeates Fontana's poetic work, [25] and Fontana aims at poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of elements. [26]

Fontana's poetic project has roots in experiments of sound poetry that began in the late 1960s [27] and is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing. [28] In this project, vocalism and writing interact with each other, creating continuously transforming areas of intervention, [29] and electronics occupies all but negligible spaces in these areas. [30]

Fontana assigns a fundamental structural role to the voice. [31] The voice mediates and catalyzes; [32] it forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. [33] In Fontana's poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. [34]

Pre-texts and Writing in Motion

Fontana's writing produces pre-texts, not texts. [35] Pre-texts are places to transfigure acoustically and visually, [36] and territories of action to be re-framed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. [37] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances [38] that are themselves capable of re-generating further writings. [39] In its typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures (hyperhyphos) beyond the page. [40] The pre-text allows new connections with text forms in the performative intermedial context, rendering work multidimensional and multidirectional, multivalent and multipotential, polycentric and multilateral, polyrhythmic and multisounding. [41]

Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has been sustaining new functions in writing that lead to the conception of text as integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages. [42]

The Poly-Artist and Hyperpoetry

The poet, in this framework, transforms into a poly-artist who takes possession of electronic, videographic, cinema, photography, the sonorous universe, theatrical dimension, and rhythmic universe. [43] The poly-artist acts poetically using all techniques, supports, and spaces, without renouncing body, gesture, and voice, which constitute the basis of a new poetic attitude. [44] The poly-artist widens and enervates the boundaries of poetry. [45] The polyartist is the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry. [46]

The poetry creates tensions by playing on contamination of systems, penetration of separate universes, and use of new media and new supports. [47] It conjugates scientific energies to energies of memory and body through a different conception of the materiality of language. [48] The poetic body undergoes subsequent processes of reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, such that one may speak of Epigenetic Poetry. [49]

Voice, Technology, and the Phono-sphere

The role of "new orality" is becoming more central in the contemporary era, [50] and the phono-sphere directly influences contemporary poetry. [51] Sound and voice are fluid and can easily cross time and space; [52] sound, mediated by modern technologies, can follow one always and everywhere. [53] The media panorama has conditioned text elaboration to a great extent. [54] Texts commonly recuperate rhythmic dimensions from 20th-century experimental techniques, street sounds, mass media scansions, and popular and archaic ways. [55]

The orality in this context is based on artificial memories of writing and on electronics, not on natural memory. [56] The sound dimension strongly influences writing in the technical context — not vocalism adhering to text. [57] Vocalism must undergo writing that is already in the phase of the poetic project. [58] It is necessary to assign a renewed function to the voice in the literary context. [59]

The voice reconnects to a long-lost orality [60] and, thanks to new tools of sound synthesis, puts itself in the position of an unheeded vocality. [61] Modern technologies highlight imperceptible sounds of the body, amplify innermost flatus, and generate new vocal universes using sophisticated software, up to total devastation of initial acoustic diagrams. [62] One can speak of an "electrophonic mask" (or "digital form") behind which sound is articulated as a fundamental aspect of language for the hyper-poetic form Epigenetic Poetry. [63] The use of confluences and digressions, conditioned and supported by vocal imprinting, may single out ultra-textual directions. [64]

Fontana's voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultra-text, body and ultra-body, animus and anima — becoming a hypervox. [65]

Poema Larsen (1983): A Case Study

In Poema Larsen (1983), the use of the microphone aims at producing and articulating sound, not just amplifying. [66] The sound is the fruit of a relation between voice, gesture, and geometric space. [67] The action gives rise to modulations of feedback caused by reactions that feed the microphones, [68] which pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units. [69] The text is limited to the word "microphone," fragmented in its phonematic components, and acts as a percussor of masses of air to put in vibration the acoustic space. [70] The interference acts as a generator of sounds, controlled by modulating gesture, [71] and the only word used is the pretext to trigger the Larsen effect, which is modulated by the action. [72] Each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds. [73]

The artist's technological sensibility is revealed in the way the artist relates to the means. [74] The more specificity is exalted, the more the range of technological signifiers is enriched in support of the work. [75]
