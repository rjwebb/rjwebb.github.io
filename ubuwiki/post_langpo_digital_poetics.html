Post-Language Writing and Digital Poetics

Post-Language Writing and Digital Poetics

Post-Language Writing and Digital Poetics is a poetic field concerned with the conditions following Language Writing and the Materiality of Language, including the emergence of digital and cybernetic publishing as a distinct poetic environment.

Spatial and verbivocovisual structure

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure. It creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality and rejects traditional linear rhythm. Its effects result from the interaction of the verbal, the visible, and the audible. (c_07485b_0005–c_07485b_0009)

The account of Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry identifies Un Coup de Dés (1897) as Mallarmé’s last major work and as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. It describes the poem’s use of page space, varying typefaces and cases, and reading indications as making the page a verbivocovisual score. (c_07485b_0014–c_07485b_0018)

Electronic, vocal, and performative poetics

In Fontana and Epigenetic Poetry, the poetic project is rooted in experiments in sound poetry and performance that began in the late 1960s. It is founded on close interaction between vocalism and writing, producing continuously transforming areas of intervention in which electronics occupy all but negligible spaces. (c_028d86_0009–c_028d86_0012)

Fontana’s writing produces pre-texts rather than texts. These pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. They generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of regenerating writings. (c_028d86_0013–c_028d86_0017)

In the hypermedia society described by the source, the voice and vocal text play central roles in poetry and prose. Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has advocated functions of writing that lead to the conception of integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hypertext, and transversal ultratext based on action languages. (c_028d86_0030–c_028d86_0032)

The pre-text’s typographical form contains metamorphic elements capable of developing into dynamic structures beyond the page. It also permits connections with performative intermedial forms, producing structures described as multidimensional, multidirectional, multivalent, multipotential, polycentric, multilateral, polyrhythmic, and multisounding. (c_028d86_0033–c_028d86_0034)

Epigenetic poetry

The source states that contamination always permeates Fontana’s poetic work, while confluences of artistic disciplines and intersections of languages distinguish it. Fontana aims at poly-dimensionality rather than a mere sum of elements. (c_028d86_0001–c_028d86_0004)

In this poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. The voice has a fundamental structural role: it mediates and catalyzes, forms and organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. (c_028d86_0005–c_028d86_0008)

The poetic body undergoes reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, a process the source calls Epigenetic poetry. The polyartist is described as the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry, using electronic, videographic, cinematic, photographic, sonorous, theatrical, and rhythmic resources while retaining the body, gesture, and voice as the basis of a new poetic attitude. (c_028d86_0035–c_028d86_0037; c_028d86_0042–c_028d86_0043)

Voice, sound, and technology

The source argues that sound and voice are fluid and can cross time and space, while modern technologies allow mediated sound to follow listeners everywhere. It describes a “new orality” as increasingly central and states that the phono-sphere directly influences contemporary poetry. (c_028d86_0019–c_028d86_0022)

This orality is based on artificial memories of writing and electronics rather than natural memory. The sound dimension influences writing in the technical domain, and texts commonly recover rhythmic dimensions from experimental techniques, street sounds, mass-media scansions, and popular or archaic forms. (c_028d86_0025–c_028d86_0028)

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone produces and articulates sound rather than merely amplifying it. Sound results from a relation between voice, gesture, and geometric space; movements model sequences of sounds, while feedback and interference generate further sonic effects. (c_028d86_0046–c_028d86_0053)

Fontana’s voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultratext, body and ultrabody, and a “hypervox.” The source further describes an “electrophonic mask,” or “digital form,” behind which sound is articulated as a fundamental aspect of language in Epigenetic Poetry. (c_028d86_0054–c_028d86_0056)
