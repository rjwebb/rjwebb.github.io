Adorno: Punctuation Marks

This page situates Adorno’s discussion of punctuation within a broader account of written language as material, spatial, visual, and sonic. The supplied framework is especially legible when read alongside concrete poetry and later experiments that treat writing as more than a linear verbal sequence. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.)

Writing beyond linear sequence

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality. It consequently destroys traditional linear rhythm. [c_07485b_0006] [c_07485b_0007] [c_07485b_0008]

In this account, the page is not merely a neutral support for language. Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dès is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. Its realization required the use of page space as an element of structure, together with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications; the page thereby becomes a verbivocovisual score. [c_07485b_0014] [c_07485b_0015] [c_07485b_0017]

This provides a useful context for considering punctuation as a feature of the written surface rather than as a purely grammatical supplement. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.)

The visual, verbal, and audible

Concrete poetry is characterized by the interaction of the verbal, the visible, and the audible. [c_07485b_0009] Joyce’s metaphor-words are presented as small verbivocovisual ideograms; the example “silvamoonlake” plays on the Latin silva (“forest”) and on the orthographic-phonetic representation of “silver-silva.” [c_07485b_0011] [c_07485b_0012]

The same concern with multiple dimensions appears in later poetic practice. Fontana’s work is distinguished by the confluence of several artistic disciplines and by intersections among languages. [c_028d86_0002] [c_028d86_0003] Its poetic game gives body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures inter-linguistic roles. [c_028d86_0005] These practices support a conception of language in which visual and sonic form participate in poetic organization. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.)

Voice, writing, and materiality

Fontana assigns the voice a fundamental structural role: the voice mediates and catalyzes, and it forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. [c_028d86_0006] [c_028d86_0007] [c_028d86_0008] The project is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing, producing continuously transforming areas of intervention. [c_028d86_0010] [c_028d86_0011]

In this model, writing produces pre-texts rather than finished texts. These pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. [c_028d86_0013] [c_028d86_0014] [c_028d86_0015] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of generating further writings. [c_028d86_0016] [c_028d86_0017]

Such claims extend the materiality of language from the printed page toward voice, performance, movement, and technology. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.) Fontana’s poetry is explicitly described as conjugating scientific energies with energies of memory and body through a different conception of the materiality of language. [c_028d86_0039]

Punctuation within a larger poetics of form

Read in this wider context, punctuation can be approached as one of the means by which writing organizes rhythm, visual spacing, and relations between verbal and nonverbal form. This is an interpretive connection rather than a claim about a particular punctuation mark. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.)

Concrete poetry’s use of space and its rejection of traditional linear rhythm offer one relevant context. [c_07485b_0006] [c_07485b_0008] Fontana’s pre-texts offer another: typographical form is said to contain “metamorphic germs” capable of achieving the complexity of later dynamic structures beyond the page. [c_028d86_0033] The pre-text can connect with performative and intermedial text forms, rendering it multidimensional, multidirectional, multivalent, multipotential, polycentric, multilateral, polyrhythmic, and multisounding. [c_028d86_0034]

The resulting emphasis is on the active organization of language by its material features: spacing, typography, sound, voice, gesture, and movement. (Interpretive framing licensed by the page rationale.)
