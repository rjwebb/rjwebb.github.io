Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry

Stéphane Mallarmé's late work occupies a foundational position in the history of concrete poetry. The essay 'Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline,' originally published in ad-arquitetura e decoraçao, São Paulo, ano IV, n. 2, March–April 1957, identifies Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dès (1897) as his last, impressive work. [1]

Un Coup de Dès as Structural Origin

Un Coup de Dès (1897) is identified as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [2] The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of "prismatic subdivisions of the Idea" cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. [3] Realizing these problems required using page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces, cases, and reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score. [4]

In this way, concrete poetry goes beyond the merely linear, introducing space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and thereby creating a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality that destroys traditional linear rhythm. [5][6]

Analogies with Other Arts

Un Coup de Dès has been described as holding for concrete poetry the same foundational role that Anton Webern holds for electronic music, [7] that Maliévitch or Mondrian hold for concrete painting, [8] and that Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier hold for modern architecture. [9]

Visually Oriented Poetry and the Ideogrammic Tradition

Visually oriented poetry can be found earlier in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the Futurists. [10] The first attempt to systematize and theorize on the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire with his calligrams; [11] yet Apollinaire's calligrams are held to demonstrate, in their failure, the insufficiency of his procedure. [12]

Concrete poetry also builds on the ideogrammic tradition. Ezra Pound gave an elementary but clear idea of the ideogram based on Fenollosa's studies of Chinese writing. [13] The Chinese define the color red with abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo, [14] and according to Pound, the Chinese ideogram for red is based on things everyone knows. [15] Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Pound [16] by introducing page space as a substantive structural element. [17]

The Verbivocovisual and Joyce

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. [18] Joyce, however, does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition, [19] instead achieving a small verbivocovisual ideogram within each of his metaphorwords — for example, silvamoonlake, which plays on the Latin silva (forest) and the orthographic-phonetic representation of silver-silva. [20] This example illustrates the "panaroma of all flores of speech" which Joyce began in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake. [21] Mallarmé's spatial-structural innovation thus stands distinct: where Joyce compresses the verbivocovisual within the single word, Mallarmé expands it across the scored architecture of the entire page. [4]
