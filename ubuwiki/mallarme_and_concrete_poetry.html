Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry

Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry is an account of Stéphane Mallarmé’s role in the spatial, typographical, and verbivocovisual development of concrete poetry.

Un Coup de Dés and spatial structure

In the article “Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline,” originally published in ad-arquitetura e decoraçao, São Paulo, ano IV, no. 2, in March–April 1957, Un Coup de Dés (1897) is identified as Mallarmé’s last impressive work. [1]

Un Coup de Dés is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [2] The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” are said to cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. [3]

The realization of these problems required the use of page space as an element of structure. Words appear in different typefaces and cases, with reading indications that make the page a verbivocovisual score. [4] In this account, concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure, creating a new rhythmic and spatial-temporal reality while destroying traditional linear rhythm. [5][6]

Ideogram and visual poetry

Concrete poetry is presented as going beyond the application of the ideogrammic process practiced by Ezra Pound. [7] Pound gave an elementary but clear account of the ideogram based on Ernest Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing. [8] In the example attributed to Pound, the Chinese define the color red through abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo; the ideogram for red is consequently based on things that everyone knows. [9]

Visually oriented poetry is also located in the work of Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the Futurists. [10] Guillaume Apollinaire’s calligrams are identified as the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem. [11] The same account holds that Apollinaire’s calligrams demonstrate, through the failure of their procedure, its insufficiency. [12]

Verbivocovisual composition and Joyce

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. [13] Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition. [14] Instead, he achieves a small verbivocovisual ideogram within each of his metaphor-words, including “silvamoonlake.” [15]

“Silvamoonlake” plays on the Latin silva, meaning forest, and the orthographic-phonetic representation of “silver-silva.” [16] The example illustrates the “panaroma of all flores of speech,” which Joyce began in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake. [17]

Analogies with other arts

The role assigned to Un Coup de Dés in concrete poetry is compared specifically with the role of Anton Webern in electronic music, Maliévitch or Mondrian in concrete painting, and Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier in modern architecture. [18][19]
