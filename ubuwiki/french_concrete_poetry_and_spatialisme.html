French Concrete Poetry and Spatialisme

French Concrete Poetry and Spatialisme is a distinct national school and named movement associated with the international concrete poetry movement.

Spatial structure

Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process associated with Ezra Pound and Fenollosa by introducing space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure.1 It creates a new rhythmic and spatial-temporal reality and destroys traditional linear rhythm.23 In Joycean terms, it results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible.4

Mallarmé and the page

Stéphane Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dés (1897) is described as his last major work and as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-time.56 The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” cover the principal areas of concern to concrete poetry.7

The realization of these problems required the use of page space as an element of structure. Words appear in different typefaces and cases, with indications for reading, making the page a verbivocovisual score.8 Un Coup de Dés is compared with Anton Webern in electronic music, Maliévitch and Mondrian in concrete painting, and Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, and Le Corbusier in modern architecture.91011

Visual and verbal precedents

Visually oriented poetry is identified in the work of Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists.12 The first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem is attributed to Guillaume Apollinaire's calligrams, although their failure is presented as demonstrating the insufficiency of the procedure.1314 Apollinaire also used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.15

Joyce's work provides another point of reference. Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition, but each of his metaphor-words can form a small verbivocovisual ideogram; “silvamoonlake” is given as an example.1617 The word combines the Latin silva, meaning forest, with the orthographic and phonetic representation of “silver-silva.”18 The example illustrates the “panaroma of all flores of speech” that Joyce began in Ulysses and realized fully in Finnegans Wake.19

Spatialisme

Spatialisme is treated here as the French national school and named movement represented by the page title. The plan for this page identifies Pierre Garnier's POSITION I manifesto and his theory of the word as object as distinct contributions to the international movement. The same plan identifies French experimental poets including Garnier, Bernard Heidsieck, Chopin, Blaine, and Bory as part of this context.
