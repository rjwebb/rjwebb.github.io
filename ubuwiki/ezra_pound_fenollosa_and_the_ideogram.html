Ezra Pound, Fenollosa, and the Ideogram

Ezra Pound, Fenollosa, and the Ideogram

Ezra Pound's engagement with the ideogrammic method—drawn from Ernest Fenollosa's studies of Chinese writing—became a foundational reference point in the theory and practice of concrete poetry, particularly for the Noigandres Group and Brazilian Concrete Poetry.

Pound's Account of the Ideogram

Decimo Pignatari's 1957 essay "Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline," originally published in ad-arquitetura e decoraçao (São Paulo, March–April 1957), provides a concise account of this lineage.[1] According to Pignatari, Pound gave an elementary but clear idea of the ideogram based on Fenollosa's studies of Chinese writing.[2] The example Pound drew upon was the Chinese definition of the color red: the ideogram is formed from abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo.[3] In Pound's reading, the Chinese ideogram for red is thus grounded in things everyone knows.[4]

This approach to meaning-making—building an abstract concept from a cluster of concrete, known particulars rather than through abstract definition—was understood by Pound as a model for poetic composition. The ideogram does not name a concept; it assembles its components.

Concrete Poetry's Extension of the Ideogrammic Method

Pignatari's essay argues that concrete poetry does not simply apply the ideogrammic process as Pound practiced it, but goes beyond it.[5] The specific innovation concrete poetry introduces is space: space enters the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure.[6] Through this move, concrete poetry creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality[7] while simultaneously destroying traditional linear rhythm.[8]

In Joycean terms, Pignatari frames this as the result of the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible.[9] He notes that Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition,[10] but that within each of his compound metaphorwords—for instance silvamoonlake—Joyce achieves a small verbivocovisual ideogram.[11] The word silvamoonlake plays on the Latin silva (forest) and the orthographic-phonetic representation of silver-silva,[12] and the example illustrates the "panaroma of all flores of speech" which Joyce began in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake.[13]

Relation to Mallarmé

Alongside Pound and Joyce, Pignatari identifies Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dès (1897) as a structural precursor.[14] Un Coup de Dès is, in this account, the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.[15] The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of "prismatic subdivisions of the Idea" cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry.[16] Realizing those problems required using page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces, cases, and reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score.[17] Pignatari draws an explicit series of analogies: Un Coup de Dès represents for concrete poetry what Anton Webern represents for electronic music,[18] what Malevich or Mondrian represents for concrete painting,[19] and what Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier represents for modern architecture.[20]

These structural precedents are examined in depth on the page Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry.

Visually Oriented Poetry as Background

Pignatari situates this tradition within a broader prehistory of visually oriented poetry, which can be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the Futurists.[21] The first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire with his calligrams,[22] although Apollinaire's calligrams are said to demonstrate in their failure the insufficiency of his procedure.[23] (Apollinaire also wrote under the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.[24])

The full theoretical elaboration of the ideogrammic inheritance within Brazilian concrete poetry is treated on the page Concrete Poetry: Theory and Method.
