International Concrete Poetry Movement

International Concrete Poetry Movement

Conceptual foundations

The article Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline was originally published in ad-arquitetura e decoraçao, São Paulo, year IV, no. 2, in March–April 1957. 1

Concrete poetry goes beyond applying the ideogrammic process as practiced by Ezra Pound. 2 It introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality. 3 In doing so, it destroys traditional linear rhythm. 4

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, “the ineluctable modality of the visible,” and “the ineluctable modality of the audible.” 5 Joyce, however, does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition. 6

Mallarmé and visual structure

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés (1897) is described as his last, impressive work. 7 It is also identified as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. 8 The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. 9

Realizing these problems required using page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score. 10 In this account, Un Coup de Dés represents for concrete poetry what Anton Webern represents for electronic music, and what Maliévitch or Mondrian represents for concrete painting. 11 It is also compared with the role of Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, and Le Corbusier in modern architecture. 12

Visual and ideogrammatic precedents

Visually oriented poetry can be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists. 13 Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem through his calligrams. 14 His calligrams nevertheless demonstrate, in their failure, the insufficiency of his procedure. 15 Apollinaire used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin. 16

Pound gave an elementary but clear account of the ideogram based on Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing. 17 According to that account, the Chinese define the color red through abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo; the ideogram is consequently based on things everyone knows. 18

Form and composition

The movement’s structural emphasis therefore places verbal, visual, and audible dimensions in interaction, while treating spatial arrangement as part of poetic construction rather than as a merely decorative feature. 19
