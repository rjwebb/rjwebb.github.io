International Concrete Poetry Movement

International Concrete Poetry Movement

The International Concrete Poetry Movement is a movement in poetry that treats spatial arrangement as a substantive element of poetic structure and develops new rhythmic, spatial-temporal forms.1

Formation and structural principles

The article Concrete Poetry: A Brief Structural-Historical Guideline was originally published in ad-arquitetura e decoraçao, São Paulo, year IV, no. 2, in March–April 1957.2 Its account states that concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Ezra Pound, Fenollosa, and the ideogram.3

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality.4 This conception destroys traditional linear rhythm.5 In Joycean terms, it results from the interaction of the verbal with “the ineluctable modality of the visible” and “the ineluctable modality of the audible.”6 The account distinguishes this practice from James Joyce, who does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition.7

The movement’s theory and method therefore involve the interaction of verbal, visual, and audible dimensions, with spatial organization treated as part of poetic construction.46

Mallarmé and spatial composition

Stéphane Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés is described as his last, impressive work and is dated 1897.8 It is identified as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.9 The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” are said to cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry theory and method.10

Realizing these problems required using page space as an element of structure. Words appear in different typefaces and cases, together with reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score.11 Un Coup de Dés is compared with the role of Anton Webern in electronic music and with the role of Kazimir Malevich or Piet Mondrian in concrete painting.12 It is also compared with the role of Walter Gropius, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, and Le Corbusier in modern architecture.13 These comparisons are treated in more detail in Vers: Une Architecture — Mallarmé, Concretism, and the Architectural Trope.

Visual and ideogrammatic precedents

Visually oriented poetry is identified in the work of François Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists.14 Guillaume Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem through his calligrams.15 The calligrams are nevertheless presented as demonstrating, through their failure, the insufficiency of Apollinaire’s procedure.16 Apollinaire used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.17

Pound gave an elementary but clear account of the ideogram based on Ernest Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing.18 According to that account, the Chinese define the color red with abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo; Pound’s explanation presents the ideogram for red as based on things everyone knows.19

Voice, sound, and media

The interaction of visible, audible, and verbal elements also provides a point of contact with sound poetry and performance. In a related account of poetic practice, Fontana and Epigenetic Poetry assigns a fundamental structural role to the voice: the voice mediates and catalyzes, and forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes.20 Fontana’s poetic project is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing, producing continuously transforming areas of intervention in which electronics occupies all but negligible spaces.21

That account states that sound and voice are fluid and can easily cross time and space; it also states that sound mediated by modern technologies can follow one always and everywhere.22 It describes pre-texts as places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and as territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms.23 These pre-texts generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of regenerating writings.24

The same account describes the poly-artist as using electronic, videographic, cinematic, photographic, sonorous, theatrical, and rhythmic resources, as well as all techniques, supports, and spaces, without renouncing body, gesture, or voice.25 It states that the poly-artist widens and enervates the boundaries of poetry, while poetry creates tensions through contamination of systems, penetration of separate universes, and the use of new media and new supports.26 The account identifies the polyartist as the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry and describes the resulting poetic body as undergoing reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, a process termed Epigenetic poetry.27
