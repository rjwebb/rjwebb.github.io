Sound Poetry and Performance

Voice, writing, and intermediality

Fontana’s poetic work is characterized by contamination, the confluence of several artistic disciplines, and the intersection of different languages.[1][2] Its aim is not to produce a sum of separate elements but to achieve poly-dimensionality.[3] Body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures take on inter-linguistic roles within this poetic game.[4]

The voice has a fundamental structural role in Fontana’s project: it mediates and catalyzes, while also forming, organizing, constructing, and re-textualizing.[5][6] The project has roots in sound-poetry experiments that began in the late 1960s and is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing.[7] These two dimensions create continuously transforming areas of intervention, in which electronics occupies all but negligible spaces.[8]

Fontana describes writing as producing “pre-texts,” rather than finished texts.[9] A pre-text is a place for acoustic and visual transfiguration and a territory of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms.[10] It generates writings in motion and poetic performances, which can in turn regenerate writing.[11] In this account, the text works with image, sound, and voice, while “new orality” becomes increasingly central.[12]

Sound and voice are fluid and can cross time and space; when mediated by modern technologies, sound can follow one everywhere.[13][14] The resulting orality is based not on natural memory but on the artificial memories of writing and on electronics.[15] The sound dimension therefore influences writing technologically, rather than functioning merely as vocalism that adheres to an already existing text.[16]

Scores and spatial-temporal form

Experimental techniques, street sounds, mass-media scansions, and popular or archaic forms contribute rhythmic dimensions to texts.[17] Text structures intended as scores, which began with difficulty at the start of the twentieth century, assume more significant roles in this context.[18] In a hypermedia society, the voice and vocal text occupy central roles in poetry and prose.[19]

This sound-oriented development has a parallel in concrete poetry’s treatment of space. Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality.[20][21] It destroys traditional linear rhythm.[22] In Joycean terms, it results from the interaction of the verbal, the visible, and the audible.[23]

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.[24] Its realization required page space to function as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications; the page thereby became a verbivocovisual score.[25] For concrete poetry, Un Coup de Dés occupies an exemplary position comparable to Anton Webern in electronic music.[26]

Visually oriented poetry also appears in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists.[27] Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem through his calligrams, although their failure demonstrates the insufficiency of his procedure.[28]

The poly-artist and the electronic voice

Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has argued for functions of writing that lead to the conception of the text as integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages.[29] In typographical form, the pre-text contains “metamorphic germs” capable of developing into complex dynamic structures—“hyperhyphos”—beyond the page.[30] It can connect with performative and intermedial text forms, becoming multidimensional, multidirectional, multivalent, multipotential, polycentric, multilateral, polyrhythmic, and multisounding.[31]

The poet consequently becomes a poly-artist who takes possession of electronic, videographic, cinematic, photographic, sonorous, theatrical, and rhythmic universes.[32] This poly-artist works through different techniques, supports, and spaces without renouncing the body, gesture, or voice, which form the basis of a new poetic attitude.[33] The result widens and intensifies the boundaries of poetry.[34] Poetry creates tensions through the contamination of systems, the penetration of separate universes, and the use of new media and supports.[35]

The voice reconnects with a long-lost orality and, through new tools of sound synthesis, assumes the position of an “unheeded vocality.”[36] The polyartist is described as the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry.[37] Because the poetic body undergoes reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, Fontana’s account names this process “Epigenetic poetry.”[38]

Poema Larsen

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone is used to produce and articulate sound, not simply to amplify it.[39] Sound arises from a relation among voice, gesture, and geometric space, while the action produces feedback modulations through the reactions that feed the microphones.[40] The microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units.[41]

The text is limited to the word “microphone,” fragmented into its phonematic components.[42] The word functions as a percussor of air masses, setting the acoustic space into vibration.[42] Interference generates sounds under the control of modulating gesture.[43] The word is thus a pretext for triggering the Larsen effect, which the action modulates; each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds.[44]

More broadly, Fontana’s voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultra-text, body and ultra-body, animus and anima, or “hypervox.”[45] Modern technologies can highlight imperceptible bodily sounds, amplify innermost bodily noises, and generate new vocal universes through sophisticated software, even to the point of destroying the initial acoustic diagrams.[46] Fontana calls the resulting technological layer an “electrophonic mask,” or “digital form,” behind which sound is articulated as a fundamental aspect of language in Epigenetic Poetry.[47]
