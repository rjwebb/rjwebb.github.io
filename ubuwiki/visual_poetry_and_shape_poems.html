Visual Poetry and Shape Poems

Visual Poetry and Shape Poems

Visual poetry and shape poems is represented by visually oriented poetry found in the work of Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and futurists. [1]

Early visual experiments

The first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire through his calligrams. [2] The failure of the calligrams is presented as demonstrating the insufficiency of that procedure. [3]

Concrete poetry and spatial structure

Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process practiced by Ezra Pound. [4] It introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality. [5] [6] It also destroys traditional linear rhythm. [7] In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from an interaction among the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. [8]

Pound gave an elementary but clear account of the ideogram based on Ernest Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing. [9] The Chinese definition of the color red uses abbreviated signs for rose, rust, cherry, and flamingo. [10]

Mallarmé and the page

Stéphane Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés (1897) is described as his last, impressive work. [11] It is presented as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [12] The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” are said to cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. [13]

The realization of these problems required using page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications. The page thereby became a verbivocovisual score. [14] Un Coup de Dés is compared with Anton Webern in electronic music, Kazimir Malevich and Piet Mondrian in concrete painting, and Walter Gropius, Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, and Le Corbusier in modern architecture. [15] [16] [17]

From the page to performance

The poetic work of Fontana is distinguished by the confluence of several artistic disciplines and by intersections among languages. [18] [19] Contamination permeates this work, whose aim is poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of its elements. [20] [21] In Fontana’s poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. [22]

Fontana assigns the voice a fundamental structural role: it mediates and catalyzes, forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. [23] [24] [25] The project has roots in sound-poetry experiments that began in the late 1960s. [26] Its vocalism and writing interact in continuously transforming areas of intervention, in which electronics occupies all but negligible spaces. [27] [28] [29]

Fontana’s writing produces pre-texts rather than texts. [30] These pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. [31] [32] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances, which can regenerate writings. [33] [34] In typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures beyond the page. [35]

Poema Larsen

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone is used to produce and articulate sound rather than merely to amplify it. [36] The sound results from a relation among voice, gesture, and geometric space. [37] The action produces modulations of feedback through reactions that feed the microphones, while the microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units. [38] [39]

The text is limited to the word “microphone,” fragmented into phonematic components; it acts as a percussor of masses of air, setting the acoustic space into vibration. [40] Interference generates sounds and is controlled by modulating gesture. [41] The single word functions as a pretext for triggering the Larsen effect, which is modulated by the action. [42] Each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds. [43]
