Visual Poetry and Shape Poems

Visual structure

Visually oriented poetry appears in the work of Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and the futurists. [1] The first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem was made by Apollinaire through his calligrams; the same account describes the calligrams’ failure as demonstrating the insufficiency of that procedure. [2] [3]

Concrete poetry extends the ideogrammic process beyond its use by Ezra Pound. [4] It introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality. [5] [6] In doing so, it destroys traditional linear rhythm. [7] Its operation is described in Joycean terms as an interaction among the verbal, the visible, and the audible. [8]

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dès (1897) is presented as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [9] [10] Realizing its problems required using the page as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications, so that the page became a verbivocovisual score. [11] [12]

From the page to multidimensional work

Fontana’s poetic work is distinguished by the confluence of several artistic disciplines and by intersections among languages. [13] [14] Contamination permeates that work, whose aim is poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of its elements. [15] [16] In Fontana’s poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. [17]

Fontana’s writing produces pre-texts rather than texts. [18] These pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. [19] [20] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances, which can in turn regenerate writings. [21] [22] In typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures beyond the page. [23]

Poema Larsen

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone is used to produce and articulate sound, rather than merely to amplify it. [24] The sound results from a relation among voice, gesture, and geometric space. [25] The action produces modulations of feedback through reactions that feed the microphones, while the microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units. [26] [27]

The text is limited to the word “microphone,” fragmented into phonematic components; it acts as a percussor of masses of air, setting the acoustic space into vibration. [28] Interference generates sounds and is controlled by modulating gesture. [29] The single word functions as a pretext for triggering the Larsen effect, which is modulated by the action. [30] Each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds. [31]
