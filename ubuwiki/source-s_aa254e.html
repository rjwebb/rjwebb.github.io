Claims

  • c_aa254e_0001 Bernard Heidsieck began creating the Poème-partition in 1955.
    In 1955, I made the difficult decision to radically change my way of writing. I titled these first texts Poèmes-partitions
  • c_aa254e_0002 In 1959, the tape recorder became Heidsieck's primary compositional and re-transmission tool.
    The purchase of a tape recorder in 1959 was a turning point in this direction.
  • c_aa254e_0003 In the 1950s in France, poetry was in a ghetto, at a dead end, and no one was reading it.
    Poetry was in a ghetto, at a dead end, and no one was reading it.
  • c_aa254e_0004 In 1955, publisher Seghers published Heidsieck's book Sitôt dit.
    In 1955, the publisher Seghers had just brought out a small book of mine called Sitôt dit
  • c_aa254e_0005 Heidsieck observed that the circulation of poetry was nil.
    I saw first hand what the circulation of poetry was: i.e. nil!
  • c_aa254e_0006 In 1955, Heidsieck decided to make poetry active by taking it out of the book and projecting it into space before an audience.
    in 1955, I made the difficult decision to radically change my way of writing ... to make it active, that is, it had to be brought out of the paper and the book and projected into space, reconnected to society, read before an audience
  • c_aa254e_0007 Heidsieck named his first texts Poèmes-partitions in reference to music, where a score is not fully alive until performed.
    I titled these first texts Poèmes-partitions, in a clear reference to music, where a work, which exists as a score [partition], is not really itself, is not fully alive, until this score is performed.
  • c_aa254e_0008 Heidsieck attended concerts of Le Domaine Musical organized by Boulez and first heard Stockhausen's Der Gesang der Jünglinge.
    I was enthusiastically attending the concerts of Le Domaine Musical that were organized by Boulez ... when I first heard a work of electronic music, namely Der Gesang Der Junglingen by Stockhausen
  • c_aa254e_0009 After hearing Stockhausen, Heidsieck thought to project words into space with poetry.
    I immediately said to myself: 'This that we have to do with poetry, project the words into the space'
  • c_aa254e_0010 Heidsieck first recorded all his Poèmes-Partitions written since 1955 after buying a tape recorder in 1959.
    I started first by recording all the Poèmes-Partitions that I had written since 1955
  • c_aa254e_0011 Tape recorders in 1959 were basic, mono, and did not allow for much.
    tape recorders at the time were very basic, mono and did not allow for much.
  • c_aa254e_0012 In 1961, Heidsieck realized he could edit recorded tape with scissors to speed up text and make collages.
    in 1961 I realized that it was possible to make changes on the recorded track with scissors (for example, to speed up a text by editing out the breaths, etc.), making collages
  • c_aa254e_0013 Heidsieck later bought a Revox A700 tape recorder, which he described as the Rolls Royce of tape recorders and a small factory.
    until I bought a new tape recorder, a Revox A700, the Rolls Royce of tape recorders, which was a small factory in itself
  • c_aa254e_0014 In the 1950s, François Dufrêne was the first to use a tape recorder for poetry in Paris, starting in 1953.
    François Dufrêne, the first, from 1953 on
  • c_aa254e_0015 In 1959, Henri Chopin, Brion Gysin, and Bernard Heidsieck began using tape recorders for poetry.
    and then in 1959, Henri Chopin, Brion Gysin and myself.
  • c_aa254e_0016 The four poets using tape recorders for poetry in Paris in the 1950s did not know each other at the time.
    there were four of us in Paris who, without knowing one another, were using tape recorders in the 1950s to 'make' poetry
  • c_aa254e_0017 Heidsieck created the Biopsies series in the second half of the 1960s, lifting elements from the body of society.
    In the second half of the 1960s I created a whole series of poems ... by lifting out elements not from the human body but from the body of society.
  • c_aa254e_0018 Heidsieck was Assistant Director of a major bank.
    professionally, I was the Assistant Director of a major bank.
  • c_aa254e_0019 Heidsieck wanted to make Poèmes Serpillieres, Poèmes Attrape-Tout, and Poèmes Caniveaux to distill the commonplace.
    I have often written that I wanted to make Poèmes Serpillieres [Mop Poems], Poèmes Attrape-Tout [Catch-All Poems], and Poèmes Caniveaux [Gutter Poems]
  • c_aa254e_0020 In 1969, Heidsieck changed the term biopsie to passe-partout because Biopsie 13 (Portrait-petales) was dedicated to a friend who died in a plane crash over the Atlantic near Dakar.
    In 1969, I changed the term biopsie to passe-partout, very simply because Biopsie 13 was dedicated to a very good friend – the poem in question was Portrait-petales – when he died, while he was learning to fly, with his instructor in a small plane, over the Atlantic near Dakar.
  • c_aa254e_0021 Passe-partout means a key that opens any door and also implies anything neutral, undifferentiated, fitting the commonplace.
    a passe-partout is a key that opens any door ... Moreover, the word passe-partout implies anything neutral, undifferentiated, without individual characteristics, which fits in with the commonplace
  • c_aa254e_0022 Canal Street is a series of 35 poems about communication.
    Canal Street (35 poems), about communication
  • c_aa254e_0023 Derviche/Le Robert is a series of 26 poems corresponding to the 26 letters, each constructed from the first ten words of the Grand Dictionnaire Le Robert whose meanings Heidsieck did not know.
    Derviche/Le Robert (26 poems corresponding to the 26 letters of our alphabet, each poem being constructed from the first ten words of the Grand Dictionnaire Le Robert dictionary whose meaning I did not know)
  • c_aa254e_0024 Respirations et Brèves Rencontres is a series of 60 poems, each a fictional meeting with a deceased poet or writer, with recordings of their actual breathing while Heidsieck speaks to them.
    Respirations et Brèves Rencontres [Breaths and Brief Encounters] (60 poems, i.e. 60 fictional meetings with poets or writers, all deceased, whose actual and authentic breathing we hear in the recordings, while I am briefly speaking to them).
  • c_aa254e_0025 Heidsieck wanted to make the poem active by taking it out of the book, standing it upright in space facing the audience.
    I had wanted to make the poem 'active', by taking it out of the book, by in some way 'standing it upright', in space, facing the audience
  • c_aa254e_0026 For Heidsieck, action in poetry consists of physically absorbing the text and projecting it into space with energy to captivate the audience.
    Action, in reality, consists in physically absorbing the text and in projecting it into the space, towards the others, breathing into it the energy that is essential for enabling it to reach and captivate the audience.
  • c_aa254e_0027 Heidsieck agrees that his sound poetry is not Dada dependent.
    I agree. Totally. But!
  • c_aa254e_0028 André Breton and Surrealism deliberately obscured Dada, which did not reappear until the 1970s with a small exhibition at the Musée d'Art Moderne in Paris.
    André Breton and Surrealism deliberately obscured Dada, which meant that it did not really begin to reappear until the 1970s, with a small exhibition at the Musée d'Art Moderne, in Paris
  • c_aa254e_0029 Heidsieck felt a reunion with Dada, finding their practice of presenting the poem live similar to his own.
    this was like reuniting with parents who had been far away ... we find that their practice of poetry, 50 years before us, was similar to the one that we were striving to establish
  • c_aa254e_0030 Dada sprang up in Zurich in 1917 to rail against the carnage of World War I and the rationalist mindset.
    Dada sprang up in a country that was neutral, in Zurich in 1917, it was with a spirit to rail against the carnage and horror that was raging in Europe ... and thus against the rationalist mindset
  • c_aa254e_0031 Dada insulted its audience to jolt it awake; Heidsieck and his contemporaries did not insult the audience, instead saying they were all in the same boat.
    Dada insulted his audience, offended it, to jolt it awake. As for us, we were not at all in similar historical conditions. We had no reason to insult our audience. On the contrary, instead, what we wanted to say to it was: 'We are all in the same boat, I have no solution, here is one that I am suggesting to you, do with it as you will.'
  • c_aa254e_0032 Dada presented simultaneous texts on stage to say everything has the same value; Heidsieck used simultaneity with tape recorder to highlight the simultaneity of sensations and challenges in daily life.
    when Dada presented simultaneous texts on stage, it was to say that everything has the same value, that nothing has any importance ... for us – and for myself, having widely practiced simultaneity, using a tape recorder – it was not at all in the same spirit, but rather out of a desire to try, using technology, to highlight the simultaneity of sensations, ideas and challenges that we experience in our daily lives.
  • c_aa254e_0033 Heidsieck does not improvise in live performances; he superimposes his live voice over pre-recorded text, which allows him to depart somewhat from it.
    Is there any improvisation in my live performances? None. Often I superimpose my own live voice with the microphone over the same (or another) text, pre-recorded on the tape ... gives me ... the possibility of departing somewhat from the pre-recorded text
  • c_aa254e_0034 Heidsieck produced Écritures/Collages in series from the 1970s onwards.
    My Écritures/Collages, which were produced, often in series, from the 1970s onwards
  • c_aa254e_0035 The first series of Écritures/Collages was Cent foules d'octobre 1970, 100 panels in one month.
    The first series was the one of Cent foules d'octobre 1970. The challenge was to produce, in one month, 100 panels of writing relating to photographs of crowds.
  • c_aa254e_0036 In 1971, Heidsieck created 40 Machines à mots, using photographs of machines.
    The following year saw the creation of 40 Machines à mots. ... I used photographs of machines, from professional magazines, and made them cough up words.
  • c_aa254e_0037 In 1974, Heidsieck bought old integrated circuits on Canal Street in New York to produce a series of Écritures/Collages, making 50 panels on communication.
    Walking on Canal Street in New York in 1974, I bought a whole series of old integrated circuits ... I made 50 panels, revolving around the general theme of communication
  • c_aa254e_0038 In July 1976, after buying a Revox, Heidsieck recorded the texts of those 50 panels, producing 35 sound poems.
    Two years later in July 1976, after buying a Revox, I took out my 50 panels again, to make a recording of their texts, which were actually summarized in 35 sound poems
  • c_aa254e_0039 The Canal Street series was released as a boxed set of 3 vinyl LPs and later as a book with 2 CDs.
    Canal Street was released as a boxed set of 3 vinyl LPs, and later as a book published by Al Dante, which included 2 CDs.
  • c_aa254e_0040 In 1989 and 1990, two printings of Circuits intégrés were published by Francesco Conz in Verona.
    In 1989 and 1990 successively, two printings of Circuits integers were published by Francesco Conz, in Verona, Italy
  • c_aa254e_0041 In 2006, Heidsieck created 19 panels Ici Radio Verona made from radio tubes.
    in 2006, 19 panels, Ici Radio Verona [Radio Verona] made out of radio tubes from old radio stations
  • c_aa254e_0042 In 2007, Heidsieck created 8 panels Radio Valescure.
    in 2007, 8 panels, along the same lines, called Radio Valescure.
  • c_aa254e_0043 In 2002, Heidsieck produced Mon Frigo a rendu l'âme, 23 panels.
    Mon Frigo a rendu l'âme, 23 panels produced in 2002
  • c_aa254e_0044 Starting in 2004, Heidsieck produced 6 different Abécédaires, each consisting of 26 panels.
    starting in 2004, the production of 6 different Abécédaires, each of which consist of 26 panels.
  • c_aa254e_0045 Heidsieck has presented 540 public readings of his texts in about twenty countries.
    He has presented 540 public readings of his texts in about twenty countries
  • c_aa254e_0046 The Abécédaires are composed of letters and fragments of magnetic tape; each letter in a series must be distinct.
    These Abécédaires [Alphabet series] are composed of letters, of course, but also often of fragments of magnetic tape. ... each Letter must be distinct from the others in the same series
  • c_aa254e_0047 Heidsieck states he is not a prophet and avoids predicting poetry's future.
    I am not a prophet and I try to stay away from predicting what could happen with respect to poetry in this new century
  • c_aa254e_0048 Heidsieck observes that pervasive computing must be exorcised and penetrated by poetry.
    pervasive computing must be exorcised, and in order to achieve this, penetrated by poetry.
  • c_aa254e_0049 Heidsieck says there are already embryonic efforts to penetrate computing with poetry, sometimes successful, sometimes disappointing; time and optimism are needed.
    There are already efforts in this direction. Embryonic. Sometimes successful, sometimes disappointing. But we have to give it time and above all remain optimistic.
  • c_aa254e_0050 Heidsieck states technology must be controlled and not used for its own sake.
    it is crucial that technology be controlled. It should not be about being consumed by it, using it merely for the sake of using it.
  • c_aa254e_0051 Heidsieck says technology should be used as a simple tool to highlight the subject.
    It should be used as a simple tool, because it is appropriate, whose purpose is to facilitate highlighting the subject that we want to address
  • c_aa254e_0052 Heidsieck warns that technology should not plunge poetry back into a new ghetto accessible only to a small elite.
    It is important that this technology, current or future, does not plunge poetry back into a new ghetto, accessible only to a small elite
  • c_aa254e_0053 Heidsieck urges preserving poetry's flow of oxygen and its ability to act as a mirror where anyone can see themselves, remaining familiar and present for everyone.
    We have to preserve poetry's flow of oxygen, its ability to act as a mirror where anyone can see themselves, and remain familiar and present for everyone, regardless of the degree of technology that it thinks or will think it should incorporate.
  • c_aa254e_0054 Bernard Heidsieck is a French poet, artist, performer, and essayist.
    Bernard Heidsieck is a French poet, artist, performer, and essayist.
  • c_aa254e_0055 Heidsieck pioneered a new form of sound-poetry through the use of 20th century recording technology and proposed the poetics of Poésie Action.
    He pioneered a new form of sound-poetry through the use of 20th century recording technology and proposed the poetics of Poésie Action.
