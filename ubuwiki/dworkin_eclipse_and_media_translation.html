Dworkin, Eclipse, and Media Translation

Dworkin, Eclipse, and Media Translation

This page treats media translation as the problem of carrying a work across material supports, and sets that problem beside accounts of spatial and electronic poetic practice. Following the plan rationale, Dworkin’s move from print to web is read as translation rather than conservation: the material aspects of a book are treated as semantically charged, while digital media are understood as rematerialising works rather than dematerialising them.

Space, rhythm, and the page

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure.[1] It creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality and destroys traditional linear rhythm.[2] In Joycean terms, it results from the interaction of the verbal, the visible, and the audible.[3]

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dès is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.[4] Its realization required page space to function as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications; the page thereby became a verbivocovisual score.[5] The work is presented as a defining precedent for concrete poetry.[6]

From pre-text to dynamic structure

Fontana’s poetic work is distinguished by contamination, by the confluence of several artistic disciplines, and by intersections among languages.[7][8] Fontana aims at poly-dimensionality rather than at the mere addition of separate elements.[9] Body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles in this poetic game.[10]

The voice has a fundamental structural role: it mediates and catalyzes, and it forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes.[11][12] Fontana’s project is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing, producing continuously transforming areas of intervention; electronics occupies all but negligible spaces in those areas.[13][14]

Fontana’s writing produces pre-texts rather than texts.[15] These pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms.[16] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of regenerating writings.[17] In typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures beyond the page.[18]

Voice, technology, and intermedia practice

Fontana’s poetic project has roots in sound-poetry experiments that began in the late 1960s.[19] In the period described by the source, the text played an important role with image, sound, and voice, while the role of “new orality” was becoming more central.[20] The phono-sphere directly influences contemporary poetry, and sound mediated by modern technologies can cross time and space and follow one always and everywhere.[21][22]

The sound dimension strongly influences writing in the technical ambit; the relevant orality is based on artificial memories of writing and on electronics rather than on natural memory.[23] Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has sustained new functions in writing that lead to the conception of integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages.[24]

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone is used to produce and articulate sound rather than merely amplify it.[25] The sound results from a relation among voice, gesture, and geometric space, while reactions feeding the microphones generate modulations of feedback.[26] The microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units.[27] Each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds.[28]

Fontana’s voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultra-text, body and ultra-body, animus and anima, and a “hypervox.”[29] Modern technologies can highlight imperceptible bodily sounds and generate new vocal universes through sophisticated software.[30] The resulting “electrophonic mask,” or “digital form,” makes sound a fundamental aspect of language in the form called Epigenetic Poetry.[31]

Interpretive frame

The page therefore places Dworkin’s archival problem alongside poetic practices in which the page, voice, electronics, and performance are not neutral containers but conditions through which works are reconfigured. This is an interpretive framing licensed by the plan rationale rather than an additional claim about the sources.
