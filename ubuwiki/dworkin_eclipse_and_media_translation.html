Dworkin, Eclipse, and Media Translation

Dworkin, Eclipse, and Media Translation

Dworkin’s account of Eclipse treats the transfer of artwork between media as a form of translation in which material form and semantic meaning are inseparable.[1][2]

Material form and meaning

For Dworkin, every material aspect of a book carries a full semantic charge.[3] The book’s physical features are therefore not merely packaging or a neutral support for verbal content. Any change of medium must attend to the formal conditions through which the work signifies.

This principle also bears on concrete poetry, which treats spatial arrangement as a substantive element of poetic structure and creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality rather than preserving traditional linear rhythm.[4][5] In Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés, page space functions as an element of structure through variations in typeface, case, and reading indications, turning the page into a verbivocovisual score.[6]

Translation between media

Dworkin argues that the attention given to form should establish the standard for translating an artwork from one medium to another.[2] Media translation consequently involves more than reproducing a work’s apparent textual or visual content: it requires negotiating the semantic consequences of changes in material support.

Digital media, in this account, do not dematerialize works. They rematerialize them, and semantic meaning is gained at the same rate at which it is lost.[1] A digital version is therefore not a transparent preservation of an earlier object but a new material configuration whose differences from the source are meaningful.

Eclipse and intermedial practice

The translation problem addressed through Eclipse can be read alongside Giovanni Fontana’s account of intermedial poetry. Fontana assigns the voice a fundamental structural role and describes an interaction between vocalism and writing that creates continuously transforming areas of intervention in which electronics occupies all but negligible spaces.[7][8] His pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms.[9][10]

In Poema Larsen (1983), for example, the microphone produces and articulates sound rather than simply amplifying it.[11] Sound arises from the relation among voice, gesture, and geometric space, while reactions feeding the microphones generate feedback modulations.[12] Such practices illustrate why a change of medium entails a reorganization of the work’s formal and material conditions, rather than a movement from materiality to immateriality.[1]
