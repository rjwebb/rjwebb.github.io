Space, visibility, and poetic structure

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure. It consequently creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality and destroys traditional linear rhythm. [c_07485b_0006] [c_07485b_0007] [c_07485b_0008]

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. [c_07485b_0009] Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition, although each of his metaphor-words can achieve a small verbivocovisual ideogram. [c_07485b_0010] [c_07485b_0011]

Mallarmé's Un Coup de Dés (1897) is his last, impressive work. It is the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. [c_07485b_0014] [c_07485b_0015] The realization of Mallarmé's problems required using page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces, cases, and reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score. [c_07485b_0017] The problems advanced by Mallarmé's theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. [c_07485b_0016]

Visually oriented poetry can also be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and futurists. [c_07485b_0021] Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem with his calligrams, although the calligrams demonstrate in their failure the insufficiency of his procedure. [c_07485b_0022] [c_07485b_0023]

From writing to sound and performance

Fontana's poetic work is distinguished by the confluences of several artistic disciplines and by intersections among languages. Contamination always permeates this work. [c_028d86_0001] [c_028d86_0002] [c_028d86_0003] Fontana aims at poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of separate elements. [c_028d86_0004]

In Fontana's poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. [c_028d86_0005] The voice has a fundamental structural role: it mediates and catalyzes, forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. [c_028d86_0006] [c_028d86_0007] [c_028d86_0008] His poetic project is founded on close interaction between vocalism and writing, creating continuously transforming areas of intervention. Electronics occupies all but negligible spaces in these areas. [c_028d86_0010] [c_028d86_0011] [c_028d86_0012]

Fontana's writing produces pre-texts rather than texts. These pre-texts are places to transfigure acoustically and visually, and territories of action to be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. [c_028d86_0013] [c_028d86_0014] [c_028d86_0015] They generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of regenerating writings. [c_028d86_0016] [c_028d86_0017]

The poetic project has roots in sound-poetry experiments that began in the late 1960s. [c_028d86_0009] In this context, the sound dimension strongly influences writing in the technical ambit, rather than vocalism simply adhering to a text. [c_028d86_0025] The orality at issue is based on artificial memories of writing and on electronics, not on natural memory. [c_028d86_0026]

The voice, technology, and expanded textuality

Sound and voice are fluid and can easily cross time and space; mediated by modern technologies, sound can follow one always and everywhere. [c_028d86_0021] [c_028d86_0022] The phono-sphere directly influences contemporary poetry, while a renewed function is assigned to the voice in the literary ambit. [c_028d86_0020] [c_028d86_0024] In the hypermedia society described by Fontana, voice and vocal text play central roles in poetry and prose. [c_028d86_0030]

Texts commonly recuperate rhythmic dimensions from twentieth-century experimental techniques, street sounds, mass-media scansion, and popular or archaic ways. [c_028d86_0028] Text structures intended as scores, which began with difficulty at the beginning of the twentieth century, assume more significant roles. [c_028d86_0029] The use of confluences and digressions, conditioned and supported by vocal imprinting, might distinguish ultra-textual directions. [c_028d86_0031]

Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has sustained new functions in writing leading to the conception of text as integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages. [c_028d86_0032] In typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures beyond the page. [c_028d86_0033] It permits new connections with textual forms in the performative intermedial ambit, rendering the work multidimensional and multidirectional, multivalent and multipotential, polycentric and multilateral, polyrhythmic and multisounding. [c_028d86_0034]

The poet becomes a poly-artist who takes possession of electronic, videographic, cinematic, photographic, sonorous, theatrical, and rhythmic universes. [c_028d86_0035] The poly-artist acts poetically through all techniques, supports, and spaces without renouncing body, gesture, and voice, which constitute the basis of a new poetic attitude. [c_028d86_0036] This widens and enervates the boundaries of poetry. [c_028d86_0037] Such poetry creates tensions through contamination of systems, penetration of separate universes, and use of new media and supports. [c_028d86_0038] It conjugates scientific energies with the energies of memory and body through a different conception of the materiality of language. [c_028d86_0039]

The poetic body as dynamic material

The poly-artist is the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry. [c_028d86_0042] The poetic body undergoes processes of reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, a process that permits the term “Epigenetic poetry.” [c_028d86_0043] The artist's technological sensibility is revealed in the way the artist relates to the means, and the more specificity is exalted, the more the range of technological signifiers is enriched in support of the work. [c_028d86_0044] [c_028d86_0045]

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone produces and articulates sound rather than merely amplifying it. [c_028d86_0046] The sound results from a relation between voice, gesture, and geometric space, while feedback is modulated by the action's reactions on the microphones. [c_028d86_0047] [c_028d86_0048] The microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units. [c_028d86_0049]

The text of Poema Larsen is limited to the word “microphone,” fragmented into phonematic components; it acts as a percussor of masses of air, putting the acoustic space into vibration. [c_028d86_0050] Interference generates sounds controlled by modulating gesture, and each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds. [c_028d86_0051] [c_028d86_0053] The single word is a pretext for triggering the Larsen effect, which is modulated by the action. [c_028d86_0052]

Fontana's voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultra-text, body and ultra-body, animus and anima, or a “hypervox.” [c_028d86_0054] Modern technologies highlight imperceptible sounds of the body, amplify innermost flatus, and generate new vocal universes through sophisticated software, extending to the devastation of initial acoustic diagrams. [c_028d86_0055] Behind this sound is an “electrophonic mask,” or “digital form,” in which sound is articulated as a fundamental aspect of language for the hyper-poetic form Epigenetic Poetry. [c_028d86_0056]
