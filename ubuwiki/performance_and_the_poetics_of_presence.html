Performance and the Poetics of Presence

Performance and the Poetics of Presence

Voice, writing, and sound

Fontana’s poetic project has roots in experiments in sound poetry that began in the late 1960s. ([1]) It is founded on a close interaction between vocalism and writing; that interaction creates continuously transforming areas of intervention. ([2]) ([3]) Electronics occupies all but negligible spaces in these areas of intervention. ([4])

The voice has a fundamental structural role in this project. ([5]) It mediates and catalyzes, and it forms, organizes, constructs, and re-textualizes. ([6]) ([7]) The project therefore assigns a renewed function to the voice in the literary ambit. ([8]) Vocalism undergoes writing that is already in the phase of the poetic project, while the sound dimension influences writing in the technical ambit rather than functioning merely as vocalism adhering to a text. ([9]) ([10])

This orality is based on artificial memories of writing and on electronics, rather than on natural memory. ([11]) The phono-sphere directly influences contemporary poetry. ([12]) Sound and voice are fluid and can cross time and space; sound mediated by modern technologies can follow one always and everywhere. ([13]) ([14]) In the era described by the source, the role of “new orality” is becoming more central. ([15])

From text to pre-text

Fontana’s writing produces pre-texts, not texts. ([16]) Pre-texts are places for acoustic and visual transfiguration and territories of action that can be reframed beyond the page in spatio-temporal terms. ([17]) ([18]) They generate writings in motion and poetic performances capable of regenerating writings. ([19]) ([20])

In typographical form, the pre-text contains metamorphic germs capable of achieving the complexity of subsequent dynamic structures—“hyperhyphos”—beyond the page. ([21]) It permits new connections with text forms in the performative intermedial ambit, rendering the work multidimensional and multidirectional, multivalent and multipotential, polycentric and multilateral, polyrhythmic and multisounding. ([22])

Since the beginning of the 1980s, Fontana has sustained new functions in writing that lead to the conception of text as integrated text, polytext in resonance, multi-poetic sonorous hyper-text, and transversal ultra-text based on action languages. ([23]) The use of confluences and digressions, conditioned and supported by vocal imprinting, might distinguish ultra-textual directions. ([24])

The poly-artist and the poetic body

The confluences of several artistic disciplines and the intersections of their languages distinguish Fontana’s poetic work. ([25]) ([26]) Contamination always permeates it. ([27]) In the poetic game, body, gesture, noises, sound, lights, colours, and architectures play inter-linguistic roles. ([28]) Fontana aims at poly-dimensionality rather than the mere sum of these elements. ([29])

The poet becomes a poly-artist who takes possession of electronic, videographic, cinematic, photographic, sonorous, theatrical, and rhythmic universes. ([30]) The poly-artist acts poetically through all techniques, supports, and spaces without renouncing body, gesture, or voice, which constitute the basis of a new poetic attitude. ([31]) This poly-artist widens and enervates the boundaries of poetry. ([32]) The poly-artist is the creator and actor of Hyperpoetry. ([33])

This poetry creates tensions through the contamination of systems, the penetration of separate universes, and the use of new media and supports. ([34]) It conjugates scientific energies with energies of memory and body through a different conception of the materiality of language. ([35]) The poetic body undergoes subsequent processes of reorganization and progressive plastic modeling, a process described as “Epigenetic poetry.” ([36])

Technology as vocal action

The artist’s technological sensibility is revealed in the way the artist relates to means. ([37]) As specificity is exalted, the range of technological signifiers supporting the work becomes richer. ([38])

In Poema Larsen (1983), the microphone is used to produce and articulate sound, not merely to amplify it. ([39]) The sound results from a relation between voice, gesture, and geometric space. ([40]) The action produces modulations of feedback through reactions that feed the microphones. ([41]) The microphones pick up phonic units and produce microphonic units. ([42])

The text of Poema Larsen is limited to the word “microphone,” fragmented into phonematic components. ([43]) The word acts as a percussor of air masses, setting the acoustic space into vibration. ([43]) Interference generates sounds controlled by modulating gesture. ([44]) The word functions as a pretext for triggering the Larsen effect, which the action modulates. ([45]) Each sequence of movements models a sequence of sounds. ([46])

Fontana’s voice in motion, sustained and mediated by modern technologies, produces sounds and draws spaces, becoming text and ultra-text, body and ultra-body, animus and anima, and finally a “hypervox.” ([47]) Modern technologies highlight imperceptible bodily sounds, amplify innermost flatus, and generate new vocal universes through sophisticated software, up to the devastation of initial acoustic diagrams. ([48]) The “electrophonic mask,” or “digital form,” is presented as a form behind which sound is articulated as a fundamental aspect of language in Epigenetic Poetry. ([49])

Concrete poetry and the spatial score

Concrete poetry introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure. ([50]) It creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality and destroys traditional linear rhythm. ([51]) ([52]) In Joycean terms, it results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible. ([53])

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dès (1897) is described as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. ([54]) ([55]) Its realization required the use of page space as an element of structure, with words in different typefaces and cases and with reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score. ([56]) The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry. ([57])

Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Pound. ([58]) Pound’s account of the ideogram draws on Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing. ([59]) Visually oriented poetry can also be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and futurists. ([60]) Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem with his calligrams, although the insufficiency of his procedure is demonstrated in their failure. ([61]) ([62])
