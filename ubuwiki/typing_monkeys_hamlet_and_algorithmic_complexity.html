Typing Monkeys, Hamlet, and Algorithmic Complexity

Typing Monkeys, Hamlet, and Algorithmic Complexity

Orientation

This page uses the figure of the typing monkey as an interpretive lens for questions about procedure, randomness, literary value, and the describability of texts. The available claims do not establish a technical comparison between Hamlet and Finnegans Wake; instead, they foreground a related problem: how literary form changes when language is treated as a material, spatial, audible, or procedurally organized system.

From ideogram to spatial structure

Concrete poetry is described as going beyond the ideogrammic process associated with Ezra Pound. It introduces space as a substantive element of poetic structure and thereby creates a new rhythmic and spatial-temporal reality, rather than preserving traditional linear rhythm. 〔c_07485b_0005〕 〔c_07485b_0006〕 〔c_07485b_0007〕 〔c_07485b_0008〕

In Joycean terms, this poetic model results from the interaction of the verbal, the visible, and the audible. 〔c_07485b_0009〕 The account distinguishes this use of space from Joyce’s practice: Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition, although each of his metaphor-words can form a small verbivocovisual ideogram. 〔c_07485b_0010〕 〔c_07485b_0011〕

The example silvamoonlake combines the Latin silva, meaning “forest,” with the orthographic and phonetic representation of “silver-silva.” 〔c_07485b_0012〕 It is presented as an instance of the “panaroma of all flores of speech,” a process begun in Ulysses and realized completely in Finnegans Wake. 〔c_07485b_0013〕

Literary value and procedural generation

The typing-monkey thought experiment is useful here not because the supplied claims establish a probability calculation, but because it raises a question about the difference between a sequence’s production procedure and its formal or literary organization. A random production process may be imagined as generating language without intention; concrete poetry, by contrast, makes spatial, rhythmic, verbal, visible, and audible relations part of the work’s structure. This is an interpretive framing licensed by the page’s rationale concerning computational poetics, procedural writing, randomness, and literary value.

On this reading, algorithmic complexity becomes a question about what must be preserved to account for a work: its words alone, or also its spacing, rhythms, visual relations, and audible dimensions. The claims support the latter possibility by treating space as structural and by defining poetic reality through the interaction of verbal, visible, and audible elements. This is an interpretation rather than a directly sourced technical result.

Mallarmé and the composed page

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés (1897) is identified as his last major work and as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality. 〔c_07485b_0014〕 〔c_07485b_0015〕 The problems raised by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” are presented as covering the principal concerns of concrete poetry. 〔c_07485b_0016〕

Realizing those problems required treating page space as an element of structure. Words could appear in different typefaces and cases, accompanied by reading indications, so that the page became a verbivocovisual score. 〔c_07485b_0017〕 In this historical-poetic account, Un Coup de Dés represents for concrete poetry what Anton Webern represents for electronic music, and what Maliévitch or Mondrian represents for concrete painting. 〔c_07485b_0018〕 〔c_07485b_0019〕

Implications for the monkey and the text

The contrast is therefore not simply between randomness and intention. It is between language considered as an undifferentiated output and language organized through multiple interacting dimensions. A typing process can be considered poetically significant, under this interpretation, only insofar as attention is given to the structures through which its output becomes readable, visible, audible, or spatially ordered. This concluding formulation is interpretive and is licensed by the rationale’s connection between procedural writing, randomness, and literary value.

The relevant background can be read alongside Mallarmé and Concrete Poetry, Concrete Poetry: Theory and Method, and Ezra Pound, Fenollosa, and the Ideogram.
