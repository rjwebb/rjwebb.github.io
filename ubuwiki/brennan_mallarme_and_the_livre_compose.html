Brennan, Mallarmé, and the Livre Composé

Brennan, Mallarmé, and the Livre Composé

Mallarmé and the spatial page

Mallarmé’s Un Coup de Dés (1897) is described as his last, impressive work.1 It is also identified as the first poetic work consciously and structurally organized according to space-temporality.2 The problems advanced by Mallarmé’s theory of the “prismatic subdivisions of the Idea” cover the main areas of concern to concrete poetry.3

Realizing these problems required using page space as an element of structure. Words appear in different typefaces and cases, accompanied by reading indications, making the page a verbivocovisual score.4 In this account, Un Coup de Dés represents for concrete poetry what Anton Webern represents for electronic music,5 what Maliévitch or Mondrian represents for concrete painting,6 and what Gropius, Mies van der Rohe, or Le Corbusier represents for modern architecture.7

Concrete poetry and the ideogram

Ezra Pound gave an elementary but clear idea of the ideogram based on Fenollosa’s studies of Chinese writing.8 Concrete poetry goes beyond the application of the ideogrammic process as practiced by Pound.9 It introduces space into the ideogram as a substantive element of poetic structure,10 creates a new rhythmic, spatial-temporal reality,11 and destroys traditional linear rhythm.12

In Joycean terms, concrete poetry results from the interaction of the verbal, the ineluctable modality of the visible, and the ineluctable modality of the audible.13 Joyce does not use the blank space of the page as an element of composition,14 although he achieves a small verbivocovisual ideogram in each of his metaphor-words, such as “silvamoonlake.”15 That word plays on the Latin silva (“forest”) and the orthographic-phonetic representation of “silver-silva.”16

Earlier visual practices

Visually oriented poetry can be found in Rabelais, Lewis Carroll, the Dadaists, and futurists.17 Apollinaire made the first attempt to systematize and theorize the visually figurative poem through his calligrams,18 but his calligrams demonstrate, in their failure, the insufficiency of his procedure.19 Apollinaire used the pseudonym Gabriel Arboin.20
